# VSL Script Framework — SOP

## Core Psychology

People do not want to watch a long video. They want:
- Fast clarity
- Clear outcomes
- Low effort decisions

A VSL is not a sales video. It is a decision engine designed to capture attention, deliver instant clarity, build belief, and drive action.

Your job is to remove friction from the decision, not "sell harder." If you delay clarity, people leave and never come back.

The goal of every section is to move the viewer from skeptical to certain to ready to buy. Every section exists to remove a specific objection or increase desire.

## Key Principles

### Belief Shifts Drive Conversions
People buy when their beliefs change. Every section of the VSL should:
- Break an old belief (what they currently think is true)
- Replace it with a new belief aligned with the offer

If the viewer's beliefs don't shift, they won't buy. Information alone doesn't convert. Changed beliefs do.

### Specificity Wins
Generic claims get ignored. Specific claims get believed.
- Use numbers, not ranges
- Use real scenarios, not hypotheticals
- Use named results, not vague outcomes

### Emotion + Logic Together
Emotion creates desire. Logic justifies the purchase. You need both in every section.
- Problem and origin story sections lean emotional
- Mechanism, teaching, and offer sections lean logical
- Proof sections deliver both

### One Core Mechanism
The best VSLs revolve around one main mechanism. Everything in the script points back to it. If the viewer remembers one thing, it should be the mechanism and why it's different.

### Attention Management
If they don't watch, nothing else matters. Throughout the script, constantly introduce:
- Curiosity (hint at what's coming next)
- Open loops (start a story or idea, resolve it later)
- New angles (don't repeat the same point, add new dimensions)

The middle of the VSL is where most people lose attention. Open loops and curiosity are how you hold them through sections 4-7.

## Structure & Timing

The VSL follows a **front-loaded summary, then deep dive** structure. The first 60-90 seconds give the viewer everything they need to decide "is this for me?" Then the rest of the video justifies that decision with depth, proof, and belief shifts.

| # | Section | Timing | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Summary | 0:00-1:30 | Front-load everything: what this is, who it's for, what problem it solves, how it works, expected outcome |
| 2 | Problem (deep dive) | 1:30-4:00 | Expand on their situation, agitate pain, describe the gap |
| 3 | Origin Story | 4:00-6:00 | Build trust and relatability through your journey: before, turning point, after |
| 4 | Unique Mechanism | 6:00-9:00 | Introduce your named, proprietary system. Explain why other methods fail and why this one works. |
| 5 | Teaching Section | 9:00-12:00 | Share 2-3 key insights that break old beliefs and introduce new ones aligned with the offer |
| 6 | Proof | 12:00-15:00 | Case studies, before/after, stacked evidence from different types of clients |
| 7 | Offer | 15:00-17:00 | Present the offer as the logical next step. Focus on outcomes, not features. |
| 8 | Objection Handling | 17:00-19:00 | Address top objections directly: time, money, complexity, doubt. Reframe into reasons to act. |
| 9 | Scarcity / Urgency | 19:00-19:30 | Limited spots, limited time, or opportunity cost of waiting. Must feel real. |
| 10 | CTA | 19:30-20:00 | Tell them exactly what to do. Reinforce the core outcome. Keep it simple. |

### Section Details

#### 1. Summary (0:00-1:30)
The summary IS the hook. No build-up. No teasing. Straight clarity.

Within 60-90 seconds, clearly state:
- What this is
- Who it's for
- What problem it solves
- How it works (high level)
- Expected outcome

People are optimizing for speed. They want to answer one question: "Is this for me or not?" If you build suspense or tease the offer, they leave. Early clarity reduces drop-off. The deep dive builds conviction for those who stayed.

#### 2. Problem Deep Dive (1:30-4:00)
Describe their current situation better than they can describe it themselves. Amplify frustration, confusion, and failed attempts.

- Mention specific failed solutions they've tried
- Highlight emotional consequences: stress, wasted time, lost money
- Make them feel understood, not lectured
- Tie back to why the status quo is unacceptable

This is where belief shift #1 happens: "My current approach is broken and I can't fix it with more of the same."

#### 3. Origin Story (4:00-6:00)
Build trust and credibility through transformation, not just credentials.

Structure:
- Before: the struggle you went through (relatable to their situation)
- Turning point: the discovery or realization that changed everything
- After: the result, with specific numbers or proof

Rules:
- Must be relevant to the offer. Don't tell your life story, tell the story that connects to the solution.
- Show transformation, not just success
- Include specific results or proof points
- Keep it tight. This is not a memoir.

#### 4. Unique Mechanism (6:00-9:00)
This is the core of the entire VSL. Without a clear mechanism, the VSL blends in with everything else.

- Explain why other methods fail (specifically, not vaguely)
- Introduce your system as the solution
- Give it a name. Make it feel proprietary.
- Keep it simple to understand. If they can't explain it back, it's too complex.

The mechanism is the #1 differentiator. If the viewer remembers one thing from the VSL, it should be this.

Belief shift: "There's a specific reason everything else failed, and this system addresses that reason directly."

#### 5. Teaching Section (9:00-12:00)
Share 2-3 key insights. Each insight should:
- Break an old belief the viewer holds
- Introduce a new belief aligned with the offer

Rules:
- Do NOT over-teach. This is not a course. It's a belief-shifting exercise.
- Keep it digestible. One insight at a time.
- Tie every lesson back to the mechanism and the offer
- The goal is to make the viewer think "this makes sense, I need this" not "now I can do it myself"

#### 6. Proof (12:00-15:00)
Remove skepticism with stacked evidence.

- Testimonials from real clients
- Case studies with before/after and specific numbers
- Personal results with proof
- Show different types of people succeeding (so the viewer sees themselves)

Rules:
- Specific is always better than general
- Before/after format is the most compelling
- Stack multiple proof points. One testimonial is not enough.
- Mix formats: quotes, screenshots, video clips, numbers

#### 7. Offer (15:00-17:00)
Present the offer as the logical next step after everything they just learned.

- Position it as the vehicle to achieve the result discussed
- Explain what they get, focused on outcomes not features
- Tie each component to a specific result
- Value framing: make the investment feel small relative to the outcome
- Risk reversal if applicable (guarantee, trial, etc.)

Avoid hard selling. If the belief shifts worked, the offer should feel like the obvious next move.

#### 8. Objection Handling (17:00-19:00)
Address the top reasons people don't buy, directly in the script.

Common objections:
- **Time**: "I don't have time for this" → Reframe: the current approach is what's eating their time
- **Money**: "It's too expensive" → Reframe: cost of inaction, what they're losing monthly by not fixing this
- **Complexity**: "This seems complicated" → Reframe: walk through how simple the first step is
- **Doubt**: "I've tried things before" → Reframe: why this is different (point back to mechanism)

Use logic + proof to address each one. Don't dismiss objections, reframe them into reasons to act now.

#### 9. Scarcity / Urgency (19:00-19:30)
Create a reason to act now instead of later.

Options:
- Limited spots (must feel real, not manufactured)
- Limited time (deadline or cohort-based)
- Opportunity cost of waiting (what they lose every month they don't act)

Rules:
- Must feel real. Fake urgency destroys trust.
- Tie urgency to outcome loss, not artificial deadlines
- Keep it short. One clear reason to act now.

#### 10. CTA (19:30-20:00)
Tell them exactly what to do. Remove all confusion.

- One action: book a call, apply, click the link
- Restate the core outcome one final time
- Keep it simple and direct

## Summary Formulas (First 60-90 Seconds)

- "This is [what it is]. If you're a [audience] dealing with [problem], here's what we do: [mechanism in one sentence]. The result is [outcome]. I'm going to break down exactly how it works and show you the proof."
- "In the next [X] minutes, I'm going to show you the exact system that [specific result] for [client type]. Here's what it is, here's who it's for, and here's what to expect. Then I'll walk you through the proof."
- "[Specific stat or result]. That's what happened when we installed [system name] for [client type]. Here's what it is and how it works. If you're a [audience] doing [revenue range], this was built for you."

## Video Chaptering

Add chapters to every VSL so viewers can skip to the sections that matter to them:
- Summary
- The Problem
- My Story
- How It Works
- Key Insights
- Case Studies / Proof
- The Offer
- Next Steps

People do not want linear content. They want control and the ability to skip. Letting people skip keeps them on the page longer, not shorter.

## Script Rules
- Write for spoken word (conversational, not formal)
- One idea per section, don't stack
- Transitions between sections should feel natural
- Include stage directions [text on screen], [cut to testimonial], etc.
- Read time: ~150 words per minute
- Every line must build curiosity, increase desire, or remove an objection. If it doesn't do one of these, cut it.
- Remove fluff. If a sentence doesn't move the viewer closer to buying, delete it.

## Common Mistakes
- Being too informational instead of persuasive. A VSL is not a tutorial.
- Over-explaining instead of creating curiosity. Leave some gaps that only the offer fills.
- Weak or vague hook. If the first 90 seconds don't give clarity, nothing else matters.
- No clear unique mechanism. Without it, the VSL blends in with everything else.
- Talking about features instead of outcomes. Nobody cares what's included. They care what it does for them.
- Not addressing objections directly. If you don't handle them in the video, they handle them by not buying.
- Losing attention in the middle. Use open loops and new angles to hold through sections 4-7.

## Length Guidelines
- Short VSL (warm traffic): 8-12 minutes (~1,200-1,800 words)
- Standard VSL (cold traffic): 18-22 minutes (~2,700-3,300 words)
- Long VSL (very cold traffic): 22-28 minutes (~3,300-4,200 words)
- Objection video: 3-5 minutes (~450-750 words)

## Real Estate / Capital Raises (real-estate-capital)
When the client's output style is `real-estate-capital`, override the default structure:
- Don't pitch the asset class broadly, max 1.5 minutes on that
- Bulk of the VSL should focus on the investment thesis, the fund, and investor FAQs
- Generic VSL is fine when not raising for a specific deal
- Target length 5-7 minutes, under 7 is ideal
- Skip heavy emotional hooks, lead with thesis and track record instead
- Skip origin story unless the founder's background directly builds investor confidence
- The standard 18-22 minute structure does not apply to this vertical
